PlainProphecy.com — Blog Writing Guidelines Who You're Writing For Primary
reader: A 16–24 year old believer who is curious but distracted, biblically
literate but not academically trained, spiritually hungry but allergic to
religious performance. They read on their phone, in short sessions, often
mid-scroll. They will leave the moment they feel talked at, talked down to, or
bored. Secondary reader: An older believer who wants depth. They'll stay if the
writing rewards them. They won't stay if it's shallow. The standard: Every post
must be good enough that a 17-year-old reads to the end and a theology student
learns something they didn't know.

The Core Writing Principle Plain doesn't mean shallow. It means clear. Your job
is to make hard things visible, not to make them small. The goal is never to
simplify the Bible — it's to remove the unnecessary friction between the reader
and the truth.

Voice Write the way you preach — but compress it. The same person who preaches
is the same person who blogs. That means: Conversational but not casual. Write
the way you'd explain something to a sharp 20-year-old over coffee. Not a
lecture. Not a tweet. A conversation where you're the one who's been thinking
about this longer. Direct but not harsh. Say the thing. Don't bury the point in
caveats. But land it with warmth, not a sledgehammer. Personal but not
performative. Use your own stories and failures the way you do in the pulpit —
to build trust, not to build a brand. Your vulnerability is always at your own
expense first. Curious, not conclusory. The best posts feel like the writer is
thinking alongside the reader, not delivering verdicts from above.

Structure — Every Post

1. Open Inside the Scene Never start with context. Never start with "In today's
   post we'll be looking at..." Drop the reader straight into something — a
   question that won't leave you alone, a cultural moment, a story, a sentence
   of Scripture that cracks open. Wrong: "The concept of righteousness by faith
   is an important one for Christians to understand..." Right: "You can't earn
   something that's already been given. That's the whole problem with the way
   most people read Paul." The first three sentences determine whether anyone
   reads the fourth.
2. Establish the Tension Every good piece of writing has a problem it needs to
   solve. State it early. Why does this matter? What's actually at stake? What
   does the reader think they know that might not be quite right?
3. Go Into the Text This is where the depth lives. Don't rush it. But don't make
   it feel like a commentary either. Move between: What the text says What key
   words actually mean (Hebrew/Greek where it matters) What the original
   audience would have heard How it fits the larger biblical story Use
   subheadings to let readers navigate. Use short paragraphs so the eye has room
   to breathe.
4. The Christ Pivot Every post must arrive at Jesus — not as a tidy conclusion,
   but as the thing the whole passage was always pointing toward. This isn't a
   formula. It's a conviction. Let it come from inside the argument, not as an
   add-on at the end.
5. Land It Close with something that does one thing: gives the reader something
   to carry. Not five action points. One question. One image. One sentence
   they'll still be thinking about on the bus home.

Depth Without Density — The Rules Rule 1: Define before you deploy. Every
technical term gets a plain English definition the first time it appears.
Righteousness. Imputed. Covenant. Eschatology. All of it. Don't assume. But
define it once, naturally — not in a glossary box. Rule 2: Hebrew and Greek
earns its place. Include original language when it genuinely changes what the
reader sees. Not to show off. When wayāśem 'al libbô does something that
"resolved" doesn't — use it. When it doesn't add anything — leave it out. Rule
3: One idea per post. The enemy of clarity is the writer who has too much to
say. Pick one idea. Say it well. Link to other posts for the rest. Rule 4:
Scholarly without being academic. You can reference ANE background, manuscript
evidence, or historical context without footnotes and hedging language. Present
findings with confidence. Flag genuine debate when it's significant. Don't pad
posts with "scholars disagree about..." Rule 5: Short sentences break the
tension. After a long, complex, multi-clause theological sentence — land short.
It gives the reader air. It makes the point stick.

What PlainProphecy Is Not Not fear-based. The goal is never anxiety, always
wonder. Not a denomination marketing tool. The theology is SDA historicist, but
the posture is open-handed. Not moralism. Never "here's what you need to do."
Always "here's what God has already done — and what that means." Not exhaustive.
A blog post is not a commentary. It's an invitation into the text, not a
substitute for it.

Formatting Sentence length: Mix short and long. Never more than three long
sentences in a row. Paragraphs: Maximum 4 sentences for normal prose. 1–2
sentences for landing moments. Subheadings: Use them. They're not decoration —
they're signposts for a reader who's scanning before they commit. Pull quotes:
One per post. The sentence the whole post builds toward. Scripture: Always cite
translation (ESV/KJV/NASB). For key texts, include the original language in
italics with a plain translation immediately after. Bold: Only for the thing
that must not be missed.

The Test Before You Publish Ask these four questions: Would a 17-year-old read
past paragraph two? If not, rewrite the opening. Is Jesus the lens or the
conclusion? If He only appears at the end, rebuild the structure. Does this post
offer something or just explain something? Information isn't enough. The reader
should leave with something they can't unknow. Could this be read as "try
harder"? If the takeaway is basically willpower with Scripture references — it
needs a gospel rewrite.
