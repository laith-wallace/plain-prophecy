The Starting Point
The Decree of Artaxerxes
457 BC · Ezra 7:11–26
457 BC
The Prophecy

"From the going out of the word to restore and build Jerusalem to the coming of an anointed one, a prince, there shall be seven weeks, then sixty-two weeks." The clock doesn't start at a vision or a prayer — it starts at a royal command. Daniel 9:25.

History Confirms

Three decrees were made concerning Jerusalem. Cyrus issued one. Darius confirmed it. But only Artaxerxes' decree — issued in the seventh year of his reign — granted authority to restore full civil and judicial government (Ezra 7:11–26). Secular historians independently date this decree to 457 BC. Ezra himself arrived in Jerusalem that same year with the king's full authority behind him.

✦ Christ at the Centre

A pagan Persian king, moved by a God he didn't fully know, issued a decree that started a 483-year countdown. It would end the moment a carpenter's son stepped into the Jordan River. God didn't need a prophet or a priest to set the clock — he used an empire.

7 Weeks · 49 Years
Jerusalem Rebuilt
457 BC → 408 BC
49 Years
The Prophecy

The first segment — seven weeks — covers the rebuilding of Jerusalem "with streets and a moat, but in a troubled time." The city would be restored. But not easily. Daniel 9:25.

History Confirms

Ezra and Nehemiah document exactly this era — the workers built with trowels in one hand and weapons in the other (Nehemiah 4:17). Surrounding nations filed legal complaints, organised military threats, and attempted to assassinate Nehemiah personally. Through all of it, the city was rebuilt. Nehemiah completed the walls in 445 BC. By 408 BC, the prophetic first segment was complete, and the long silence began.

✦ Christ at the Centre

The stones Nehemiah's workers laid under threat of violence were the same streets Jesus would walk 400 years later. Every rebuilt gate, every restored wall was preparation — God building the city where the triumphal entry would happen, where the last supper would take place, where the cross would stand.

62 Weeks · 434 Years
The Long Silence
408 BC → AD 27
434 Years
The Prophecy

"And after the sixty-two weeks, an anointed one shall be cut off and shall have nothing." The long wait. Four centuries of prophetic silence. Then — on schedule — the Anointed One. Daniel 9:26.

History Confirms

Between Malachi and Matthew there are 400 years with no prophet, no revelation, no word from God. Persia gives way to Greece. Alexander marches. Four generals split his empire. The Seleucids and Ptolemies war over Judea. Antiochus Epiphanes defiles the temple. Rome rises and occupies the land. Then, in AD 27 — exactly 434 years after 408 BC — a man from Nazareth walks out of Galilee. Luke is careful to give us the date: the fifteenth year of Tiberius Caesar (Luke 3:1).

✦ Christ at the Centre

Jesus didn't arrive approximately on time. He arrived on schedule. When he stepped out of the water at his baptism and said "The time is fulfilled" (Mark 1:15), that wasn't a vague claim. It was a direct announcement to anyone who knew their Daniel: 483 years just ended. The Messiah is here.

483 Years Fulfilled
The Anointed One Arrives
AD 27 — Jesus Baptised · First Half of Final Week
AD 27
The Prophecy

"Until Messiah the Prince." The word "Messiah" in Hebrew means Anointed One. So does "Christ" in Greek — Christos. The 69 weeks (483 years) pointed to a specific moment: the arrival and anointing of this person. Daniel 9:25.

History Confirms

Luke 3:1–22 is one of the most historically anchored passages in the New Testament. The fifteenth year of Tiberius Caesar places Jesus' baptism firmly in AD 27. At that moment, the Holy Spirit descended on Jesus physically — a literal anointing — and a voice from heaven declared him God's Son. He immediately announced: "The time is fulfilled, and the kingdom of God is at hand." (Mark 1:15). The 483-year countdown from 457 BC ends in AD 27. Not approximately. Exactly, accounting for the lack of a year zero.

✦ Christ at the Centre

"Christ" is not Jesus' last name — it's his title. The Anointed One. When the Spirit descended at the Jordan, he was being anointed exactly as Daniel had predicted, exactly when Daniel had predicted it. Six centuries of prophecy compressed into one river, one dove, one voice from heaven.

The Final Week · AD 27–34
Covenant Confirmed
Cross (AD 31) · Gospel to Gentiles (AD 34)
7 Years
The Prophecy

"He shall confirm the covenant with many for one week, and in the middle of the week he shall put an end to sacrifice and offering." The Messiah would confirm the covenant for a full seven years — and be cut off at the midpoint. Daniel 9:27.

History Confirms

Jesus ministered for exactly 3.5 years before his crucifixion in AD 31 — the mathematical midpoint of the 70th week. The moment he died, the temple curtain tore from top to bottom (Matthew 27:51): a sign that the sacrificial system was finished. Three and a half years later, in AD 34, Stephen became the first Christian martyr (Acts 7). The persecution that followed scattered believers throughout Judea and Samaria — fulfilling Acts 1:8 and closing the 490-year covenant period with the Gospel going to the Gentile world.

✦ Christ at the Centre

"He shall put an end to sacrifice and offering." Every animal on every altar, from Abel to the temple, had been pointing forward. In AD 31, the thing they were all pointing at arrived. The curtain didn't tear by accident — it was torn from top to bottom, heaven's way of saying: this is finished. He is the offering. There is nothing left to add.
